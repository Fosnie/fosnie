# Research methods

How to investigate a question well and report it honestly. This shapes *how you
reason*, not the file you produce.

## Structure the investigation

1. **Sharpen the question.** State what is actually being asked, the scope, and the
   decision it serves. Note unstated assumptions and ambiguities; surface them rather
   than guessing.
2. **Decompose.** Break the question into sub-questions you can each answer from
   evidence. Identify what *would* change the answer (the load-bearing facts).
3. **Plan the search.** Decide what sources could answer each sub-question and in what
   order — primary sources and the corpus first, secondary commentary after.
4. **Iterate.** Let findings reshape the plan; chase the contradictions, not just the
   confirmations.

## Weigh sources (source criticism)

- **Provenance** — who produced it, when, why, and for whom? Primary (the instrument,
  the filing, the dataset) outranks secondary (commentary about it).
- **Currency** — is it the latest position? Note the date; flag superseded material.
- **Corroboration** — is a claim supported by independent sources, or echoed from one
  origin? Distinguish *many sources* from *many copies of one source*.
- **Conflict** — when sources disagree, say so explicitly; present the disagreement and
  weigh it, don't silently pick one.
- **Bias & limits** — note conflicts of interest, sample limits, and what the source
  cannot tell you.

## Citation discipline

- **Every non-obvious claim carries a citation** to the source that supports it.
- Cite the **specific** source (and locator — section/page) for each claim, not a
  general bibliography dump.
- Quote sparingly and exactly; paraphrase faithfully; never inflate what a source says.
- Keep citation numbers stable through the write-up and list them at the end.
- **Say what you don't know.** Mark gaps, unverified claims, and "not addressed by the
  available sources" explicitly — an honest gap beats a confident guess.

## Synthesis

- Answer the question first, then support it; lead with the conclusion the evidence
  best supports.
- Separate **what the sources say** from **your inference**; label inference as such.
- Note the confidence and what would raise or lower it.

## Why this matters

In legal and financial work a research output is acted upon. A missing citation, an
overstated source, or a buried disagreement is a real liability — rigour and honest
uncertainty are the product, not decoration.
